I finally finished the MVP for [bote](https://github.com/jankdc/bote). What turned out to be a silly idea to test out agentic coding turned into a 6 month endeavour in making something I'm proud of. Mind you, it wasn't a straight path to get here. There were basically 3 other projects that went nowhere to get to this point. I can say that I've learnt from them now but if I didn't come out of that tunnel vision of a hole then I would've felt very differently.

I think at some point, I got addicted to AI. I'm a fairly capable software engineer but only at specific domains of the craft. I'm so used to solving problems in those domains that I struggled to pivot into something new and uncomfortable because the time it takes to learn something novel is not smooth anymore like it used to feel, like at university or when you're on the job. So when something like an exo-skeleton of a tool gives you the power to wield those out-of-reach knowledge and algorithms without writing them yourself, it felt exciting. 

What's interesting about these agents is that in the span of 6 months (2025-2026), it felt like I was witnessing a child grow up. It went from complete hand-holding to now telling me that my ideas are terrible. I realise that what's giving me confidence in these things is not that it can execute my ideas, but whether it can criticise and collaborate with me. I don't want a yes-man to enable the sunk-cost fallacy of having me finish something that isn't really useful to anyone. I learn and motivate best when it strikes a balance of making something for myself and for someone else.

A lot of the effort in collaborating with AI is that you have to check the code yourself, sharpening your radar for slop and if possible, creating tools to automate that slop-detection. Ideas and execution are now cheap, so verification of quality is now more expensive. The AI can happily write more complicated code for you if you leave it unchecked. There's a level of trust with human co-workers. You've been working with them for awhile, figured out their styles and quirks, strengths and weaknesses, basically the context to adjust the level of focus on verifying their work. With AI, at least for the cloud ones, you never know what you're gonna get so you tend to be more vigilant on what they produce.

I learnt a lot in this project, but I fear that that what I've learnt isn't transferrable. For example, I learnt a lot about Rust, SIMD and bitmap construction, but if my robot co-worker gets taken from me, will I be able to re-create what I've done? Is it really my work if I can't reproduce it by myself? Can I really be bothered fighting with Rust's ownership rules on my own? It's kind of similar to vaping right now: no one really knows the long-term effects of it until everyone's old enough to get a sample size. One thing I'll say though is that reading enough Rust or SIMD has given me enough of a heuristic to what's good or not, based on my own fundamental experience in making software and managing complexity.

I was supposed to write about what mistakes and lessons I've learnt in detail here, partly because there's a part of me that felt like I've wasted 6 months of playing around with AI and making something that I can't quite attribute to myself and wanted something to show for it. Now it kind of ended into a retrospective how I felt, and how this is all giving me imposter syndrome.

Going forwards, I think being surrounded by incredible people or tools is an opportunity to get help in doing something I envisioned but I think in striving for self-improvement and developing tastes, I should also do the thing along with it or at least develop a mental model of what's going on, to apply a better management of quality. Anyway, I spent half a year dedicated to this and used a lot of my savings, I should probably get a job...

*looks at job market*...**sigh**
